BimmerUS - Ecommerce School Project

Services

Research, Problem Articulation, Storyboarding, Information Architecture, Interaction Design, Wireframe Production, UI Development

The Project Objective

The objective of this project was simple. I was to create a fictional company and design and develop a live mock of their ecommerce website, while utilizing all the core design practices I had learned over the past year. I began the project by selecting the type of product I wanted sell. I wanted to be fairly specific due to the quarters’ time constraint. I elected to design a site by the name of BimmerUS.com, a BMW auto parts warehouse for the do-it-yourself type. From there, I began to analyze the industry, the competitors and the typical user that may venture onto the site. From this analysis, I planned three key features, which include the ability to filter to your specific vehicle, a sliding banner advertising the product, as well as a touch screen compatible product sliders. I felt as if these features would help ease the process of selecting the products and allow the user to quickly move through the site on into the checkout process.

Defining the Project

Research

When considering the quantity of product ecommerce sites tend to stock, especially in regard to auto parts, I knew that the my design needed to provide the user with enough product per page to meet their wants and needs, while being sure not to overwhelm them with too much at any given time. I began by researching well-established brands such as Amazon, Walmart and Costco to name a few. Each company used very popular web conventions such as the shopping cart, search bar, sign in, wish list, etc. In addition to web conventions, pages were broken up into clearly defined areas from the product link to ads for “today’s deal.”

Creating the Information Architecture

I knew the Information Architecture would be bit of a challenge to arrange in a manner that delivered access to each product, while remaining simple to navigate. I needed to appeal to the “browse first, ask later” user, in addition to the search dominant user. I concluded that the bottom up approach would be best suited for this endeavor. I began by creating the company inventory, which I began grouping together by major component such as engine, transmission and suspension. Each of these categories could be accessed from the individual pages, in addition to the utility options. I felt as if this strategy would allow first time visits along with the subsequent visits to remain swift. As the user progressed deeper into the site, especially in regard to the shopping cart, I implemented a breadcrumb navigation in order for the user to know exactly where they were at all times.

Creating the Interaction Design

I wanted the checkout interaction design experience to match the users' expectations. I chose to stick with the well-known naming convention and sequence of steps. As you can see from the diagram below, each page answers a simple yes or no question, which will direct the user to the next logical step. For instance, if a user did not have an account and would like to avoid creating an account, they are able to easily opt out of the sign up process and continue to the billing/shipping information page where they can then place their order and finally review the order. Users are so accustomed to the constancy of shopping carts on the web today, that swaying to far from that workflow could cause more headache than what it is worth.

Wireframes

When designing the wireframes for BimmerUS, I was really able to focus on the relationships and functionality between the elements on the page. I wanted to establish a sort of hierarchy as it pertained to the product. For instance, on the home page I placed large banners and advertisements near the top in order to catch the users eye as they entered the site. This strategy would allow users to see and hopefully purchase product that they didn’t come to the site for originally. Once the user entered the site, the filter and advertisements would change based off of the selected product. This step really allowed me to hammer out the navigation and how I would present the entire product line BimmerUS offers. The site was built on a 960px 12-column grid, which allowed me to distribute product evenly across the page.

Color Palette

I wanted the site to appeal to the focus audience, males ranging from 25 to 35. A study done in 2003 by Joe Hallock compared color preferences among males and females and that study showed 57% of men saying blue was their favorite color. Taking this into consideration, I chose a color similar to the BMW blue. Since the product BimmerUS offered was exclusive to BMW, I wanted the color palette to also reflect the brand. My goal was to reflect the quality and standard BMW has established over the years and portray that image to the customer.

Typeface

During my search I considered 3 key aspects of a typeface: readability, visual hierarchy, function. I wanted a typeface that had the same clean look as Helvetica, which BMW had a version of. I stumbled upon Museo Sans which, had many similarities to that of Helvetica. Limiting my typeface choice to just one selection helped establish the hierarchy and tone I was seeking. I was able to manipulate the font-size to highlight areas I wanted users to focus on. For instance, banner ads had much larger font sizes than that of headings and descriptions. Though the typeface wasn’t relied upon to meet the overall goal of selling product, it was an integral part of the design.

Visual Design

Structure, color, spacing, symmetry, and the amount of text were all carefully considered when building the visual design. I wanted the site to remain simple and familiar in order for customers to feel comfortable moving about the pages. I want them to feel as if they had been there before, even if they were first time visitors. I began by researching eye-tracking studies to carefully place elements in strategic locations, in turn allowing the user to move about the page seamlessly. When deciding upon content to place above the fold, I concluded that ads and specials should take precedence. I concluded that this strategy would improve overall sales and move older product faster in order to make room for the new.
